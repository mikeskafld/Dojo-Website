## The Problem with Traditional Online Courses

We've all been there: you sign up for an online course, excited to learn a new skill. The first week goes well. By week two, you're behind. By week three, the course sits untouched in your browser tabs, a monument to good intentions gone astray.

The completion rate for traditional online courses? A dismal 5-15%. That's not a learner problem—it's a design problem.

## Enter Micro-Learning

Micro-learning breaks content into 5-10 minute lessons that respect how your brain actually works. It's not a shortcut or a dumbed-down version of "real" learning. It's actually more effective, and the science backs this up.

### Cognitive Load Theory

Your working memory can only hold so much information at once. Cognitive psychologist John Sweller discovered that overwhelming learners with too much information at once leads to poor retention and increased frustration.

Traditional courses dump hours of content on you at once. Micro-learning delivers one concept at a time, allowing your brain to:

- Process information fully before moving on
- Build strong neural pathways for each concept
- Avoid the "forgetting curve" that happens with information overload

### The Spacing Effect

Hermann Ebbinghaus discovered that we retain information better when learning is spaced out over time rather than crammed into long sessions. This "spacing effect" is why cramming for exams rarely leads to lasting knowledge.

Dojo's micro-lesson format naturally creates spacing. When you learn for 5-10 minutes at a time, you're forced to return multiple times, reinforcing neural pathways with each session.

### Active Recall

Passive consumption (reading, watching) creates an illusion of learning. True retention requires active recall—actually using and applying what you've learned.

Each Dojo lesson is designed around this principle:

1. **Learn** - A focused concept is introduced
2. **Practice** - You immediately apply it
3. **Reinforce** - The next lesson builds on what you've mastered
